The Mononchida are an order of predatory nematodes characterized by a distinct set of morphological features. Here's a breakdown of their key anatomical aspects:

  • Body: Mononchids are typically elongated and slender nematodes, with a smooth and cylindrical body. Their size can vary depending on the species, but they generally range from a few millimeters to several centimeters in length.
  • Cuticle: The body wall of Mononchids consists of a tough, multi-layered cuticle that provides protection and support.
  • Mouthparts: One of the defining features of Mononchida is their unique mouthpart structure. They possess a modified and muscular pharynx, known as a probola, which functions as a lethal weapon for capturing prey. The probola can be everted (pushed outward) to pierce the bodies of their invertebrate prey, injecting paralyzing venom and digestive enzymes.
  • Stiletto: Within the probola, some Mononchida species have a sclerotized structure called a stiletto. This acts like a sharp spear, further aiding in penetrating the prey's body cavity.
  • Digestive System: The digestive system of Mononchids is relatively simple. After the probola paralyzes and liquefies the prey, the nutritious fluids are then sucked into the nematode's gut through the probola.
  • Reproductive System: Mononchids are dioecious, meaning there are separate sexes. Males typically have a single spicule, a copulatory organ used for sperm transfer. Females have paired ovaries and uteri for egg production.
  • Sense Organs: Mononchids have various sensory organs, including cephalic papillae (sensory bumps) located around the head for touch and chemoreception. Some species may also possess amphids, chemosensory organs that aid in detecting prey and environmental cues.

Overall, the morphology of Mononchida reflects their predatory lifestyle. Their slender body allows for easy maneuvering in soil and water, while their specialized mouthparts make them efficient hunters of other small invertebrates.
