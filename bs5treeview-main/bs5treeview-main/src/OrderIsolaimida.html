Isolaimida are a relatively obscure order of nematodes, but their morphology has some interesting characteristics. Here's a breakdown of their key features:

  • Size and shape: They are long and slender nematodes, typically ranging from 3 to 6 millimeters in length. Their bodies are cylindrical and smooth, with a pointed head and a short tail.
  • Cuticle: The outer covering of their body, called the cuticle, may have punctations or markings. These markings are most noticeable on the tail, where they can appear in longitudinal and transverse rows.
  • Sensory organs: Amphids, which are sensory organs found in many nematodes, seem to be absent in Isolaimida.
  • Feeding apparatus: Their stoma, the muscular mouthpart used for feeding, is a unique feature. It's very long, tubular, and has a triradiate (three-rayed) structure. The walls of the stoma are thickened in the anterior (front) part, likely for grasping or piercing food.
  • Reproductive system: Females have a diovarial reproductive system, meaning they have two ovaries. They are also amphidelphic, with both ovaries connected to a single oviduct. In some species, the ovaries may be reflexed, meaning they loop back on themselves. Males have paired testes.
  • Spicules and gubernaculum: Spicules are paired structures in male nematodes that aid in copulation. In Isolaimida, the spicules are thick and well-developed. The gubernaculum, another structure involved in mating, is small.
  • Tail: The tails of both sexes are short and relatively simple. They can be conical (cone-shaped) or bluntly rounded with pointed tips. Males have large, paired caudal papillae on their tails, which are sensory structures. Females have small paired papillae on their tails as well.
  • Habitat and diet: Isolaimida are mainly found in undisturbed sandy soils. Their diet is believed to consist mainly of bacteria, but they may also be carnivorous as some specimens have been found with bristles of oligochaetes (a type of worm) in their intestines.
