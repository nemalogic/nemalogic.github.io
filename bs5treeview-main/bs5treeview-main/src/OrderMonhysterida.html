The Monhysterida are an order of nematodes characterized by a distinct body morphology. Here's a breakdown of their key features:

  • Body size: They are generally small nematodes, with most species ranging from 0.3 to 1.0 mm in length.
  • Body shape: Their body is elongated and cylindrical, with a tapering end at the tail.
  • Cuticle: The body is covered by a thick, four-layered cuticle that provides protection and support.
  • Mouth: The mouth is located at the anterior end of the body and is surrounded by six lips. These lips aid in feeding by grasping prey or scraping detritus.
  • Pharynx: The pharynx is a muscular organ that pumps food into the intestine. In Monhysterids, the pharynx is usually muscular and may have a sclerotized (hardened) pump chamber.
  • Esophagus: The esophagus is a narrow tube that connects the pharynx to the intestine.
  • Intestine: The intestine is a long, winding tube that absorbs nutrients from food.
  • Reproductive system: Monhysterids are dioecious, meaning there are separate sexes. Males have a single testis and a spicule, which is a copulatory organ. Females have two ovaries and a vulva located in the middle of the body.
  • Tail: The tail is short and blunt in most Monhysterida species.

Some additional points to note about Monhysterid morphology:

  • They lack cephalic setae (sensory bristles on the head).
  • They have a well-developed deirid (a sensory organ) located near the midbody.
  • They have phasmids (another type of sensory organ) located near the anus.
  • Their spicules (male reproductive organs) are typically paired and symmetrical.

The specific morphology of some organs, like the shape of the spicules or the structure of the deirid, can vary between different Monhysterid genera and species. These variations are often used for taxonomic identification.
