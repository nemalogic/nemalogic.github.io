Dorylaimina, a suborder within Chromadoria, possesses some unique morphological features alongside characteristics common to Chromadoria. Here's a breakdown of what defines Dorylaimina morphology:

  • Cuticle: Similar to Chromadoria, Dorylaimina have a cuticle, but it can be smooth or striated (with faint lines) in some species.
  • Lip region: The head region, or lip region, in Dorylaimina has two distinct whorls of papillae (sensory structures). The inner whorl typically has 6 papillae, while the outer whorl has 10.
  • Amphids: These chemosensory organs come in various shapes within Dorylaimina. They can be stirrup-shaped, cyathiform (cup-shaped), or saccate (pouched), and have pore or slit openings.
  • Esophagus: The esophagus in Dorylaimina is a two-part muscular structure. The anterior part is slender, while the posterior part is swollen and glandular, containing muscles for food manipulation.
  • Odontostyle: A key feature of Dorylaimina is the presence of a movable, hollow spear called the odontostyle. This specialized structure originates from a migrated stomal cell and is used for feeding by puncturing prey or plant tissues.
  • Cheilostome: The cheilostome is a structure associated with the odontostyle. It includes a vestibule (chamber) and guiding apparatus that helps in the protrusion and retraction of the spear. In some Dorylaimida, this guiding apparatus may appear as a single or double ring around the odontostyle.
  • Esophageal glands: Five unicellular esophageal glands are present in the basal part of the esophagus in Dorylaimina. These glands secrete enzymes that aid in digestion.
  • Cardia: A muscular valve, the cardia, is present at the junction of the esophagus and intestine, regulating the flow of food.
  • Nerve ring: The nerve ring, which coordinates the nervous system, surrounds the slender anterior part of the esophagus in Dorylaimina.

Overall, the morphology of Dorylaimina reflects their predatory or omnivorous feeding habits. The specialized spear (odontostyle) and muscular esophagus are adaptations for capturing and processing food. Additionally, the specific arrangement of papillae and amphid shapes likely play a role in their sensory perception within their environment.
