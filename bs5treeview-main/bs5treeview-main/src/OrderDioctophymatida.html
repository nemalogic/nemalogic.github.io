The Dioctophymatida are an order of parasitic nematodes known for their large size and red color. Here's a breakdown of their morphology:

  • Size: These are some of the biggest nematodes, with females reaching lengths of up to 100 cm, while males are smaller. For instance, Dioctophyma renale, a parasite that infects the kidneys of minks and ferrets, can have females this large.
  • Color: Dioctophymatida are typically a red color, which is due to the presence of hemoglobin, a protein that carries oxygen in red blood cells. This hemoglobin is likely acquired from their hosts.
  • Intermediate Hosts: Their life cycle involves an intermediate host, which is always an oligochaete annelid (segmented worm).
  • Eggs: Dioctophymatida eggs have a distinct feature - thick shells with one or two polar opercula (lids) at each end.
  • Males: Male Dioctophymatida can be identified by their bell-shaped bursa, a structure near the tail end that aids in mating, and a single spicule, the reproductive organ in male nematodes.
