The Nygolaimoidea are a superfamily of nematodes, which are roundworms. They are typically larger than 1 millimeter long. Here's a breakdown of their key morphological features:

  • Body wall: The cuticle, or outer body wall, of Nygolaimoidea is smooth or has fine lines running across its width.
  • Head: The lip region is not very distinct and the lips are fused together.
  • Mouth: The mouth, or stoma, has a muscular structure called a mural tooth on the lower wall of the pharynx.
  • Esophagus: The esophagus, a muscular tube that connects the mouth to the intestine, has two sections. The front part is slender and narrows as it goes down. The back part is wider and takes up about half the total length of the esophagus.
  • Intestine: The junction between the esophagus and the intestine has three large intestinal cells.
  • Reproductive system:
    • Females: Most females have two ovaries (didelphic), but some have only one ovary located in the back half of the body (mono-opisthodelphic). Their reproductive organs are not well-developed, and the vagina usually lacks a specific hardened section. The vulva, the opening of the vagina, is typically horizontal but can be vertical in some species.
    • Males: Males have two testes and well-developed spicules, which are hardened structures used in reproduction. They also have lateral guiding pieces to help maneuver the spicules and sometimes have additional structures like a gubernaculum and supplements.
  • Tail: The tail varies in shape and size but is generally similar between males and females.

Overall, the Nygolaimoidea have a relatively simple body structure compared to other nematodes.
