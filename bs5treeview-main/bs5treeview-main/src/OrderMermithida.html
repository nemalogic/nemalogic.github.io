The Mermithida, also known as hairworms or nematode-hairworms, are a phylum of parasitic roundworms. Their morphology is well-adapted for their parasitic lifestyle. Here's a breakdown of their key features:

  • Body: Mermithids have long, slender, cylindrical bodies. They lack segmentation and have a smooth cuticle, which can be transparent or opaque depending on the species. Their body size varies greatly depending on the species, with some reaching lengths of up to 2 meters (over 6 feet)!
  • Symmetry: They exhibit bilateral symmetry, meaning their left and right sides are mirror images of each other.
  • Mouth and Digestive System: Their mouth is located at the anterior (front) end and is surrounded by sensory papillae. They have a muscular pharynx that helps them suck fluids from their hosts. Interestingly, adult mermithids lack a functional digestive system as they don't feed during their free-living adult stage. They rely on the nutrients they accumulated during their parasitic larval stage.
  • Reproductive System: Mermithids are dioecious, meaning there are separate sexes. Males are typically smaller than females. Fertilization is internal.
  • Lack of external segmentation: Unlike many other nematodes, mermithids don't have external segmentation on their bodies.
  • Nerve ring: They have a nerve ring located near the anterior end, which coordinates their nervous system.
  • Excretory system: They possess a simple excretory system consisting of paired canals that run along the length of their body and expel waste products through pores.

It's important to note that there is some variation in morphology among different mermithid species. However, the features mentioned above are characteristic of the phylum as a whole.
